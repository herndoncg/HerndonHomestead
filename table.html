The Herndon Table – Hospitality & Meals.

“The Herndon Table is where faith, food, and family gather. We cook, share, and open our home to all.”

Welcome to our table

Here, supper is more than a plate of food—it’s a daily rhythm of prayer, conversation, and care. We slow down, look one another in the eye, and remember that people come before pace.

We cook simple, seasonal meals: cast‑iron sears, garden sides, fresh bread, and pies that bring everyone back for seconds. We pass plates, share stories, and teach our kids the quiet skills of gratitude, kindness, and serving one another.

Whether it’s a weeknight skillet or a holiday feast, there’s always room for one more. If you’re near, pull up a chair; if you’re far, cook along with us—we hope these recipes and practices make hosting feel natural and joyful.

The Benefits of Coming Together

Family Before Frantic

We slow down to look one another in the eye, say grace, and share stories. The table anchors our home and reminds us that people matter more than pace. We sit and speak of God’s words “when you sit in your house” (Deut. 6:6–7) and remember that it’s “better a simple dinner with love than a feast with strife” (Prov. 15:17).

Breaking Bread Builds Hearts

Cooking and eating together forms gratitude, generosity, and belonging. The early church “broke bread in their homes and ate together” with glad hearts (Acts 2:46), and Jesus was “known… in the breaking of the bread” (Luke 24:30–31).

Hospitality Is Ministry

Opening our doors is how we live our faith—welcoming friends and strangers, sharing what we have, and making room at the table. We “contribute to the needs of the saints and practice hospitality” (Rom. 12:13) and “do not neglect to show hospitality to strangers” (Heb. 13:2).

Conversation That Disciples

We talk about our day, our hopes, and God’s Word. Little, regular conversations shape character and pass on our values—“teach them diligently… and talk of them when you sit in your house” (Deut. 6:6–7)—and we aim that “whether you eat or drink, do all to the glory of God” (1 Cor. 10:31).

Scriptures We Stand On

This table is anchored in a few simple verses: Acts 2:46 (sharing meals with glad hearts), Deuteronomy 6:6–7 (teaching God’s Word at home), Romans 12:13 (practicing hospitality), and 1 Corinthians 10:31 (doing all to God’s glory).

Cooking Techniques

Beginner Start here to build confidence
Beginner

Heat Zones & Sear

Preheat the pan 3–5 minutes. Test with a drop of water (it should dance). Add oil, then meat. Don’t move it for 90–120 seconds.

Beginner

Seasoning Cast Iron

Coat lightly with neutral oil, bake at 450°F for 1 hour, cool in oven. Repeat 2–3× for a durable, nonstick patina.

Beginner

Knife Skills & Mise

Keep a sharp 8" chef’s knife. Group prep (mise en place) before cooking to make dinner smooth and stress‑free.

Beginner

Roasted Vegetables

Sheet pan at 425°F, don’t crowd. Toss veg with oil/salt. Roast until edges char and centers turn tender‑sweet.

Beginner

Thermometer 101

Use an instant‑read. Pull chicken at 160–162°F (rest to 165°F); steak at target minus 5°F to account for carryover.

Intermediate Flavor building & better textures
Intermediate

Pan Sauces 101

After searing, deglaze with stock or wine, reduce, then whisk in 1–2 tsp cold butter off heat for gloss and body.

Intermediate

Braising Basics

Brown meat, add aromatics, deglaze, then cook covered at 300°F with liquid 1/3–1/2 up the sides until fork‑tender.

Intermediate

Southern Gravy & Biscuits

Use rendered fat + flour for a light roux, whisk in milk, simmer till nappé. Split hot biscuits; serve immediately.

Intermediate

Oven‑“Fried” Crunch

Wire rack on a sheet pan, high heat (425–450°F), and panko + grated cheese for crisp without deep frying.

Intermediate

Fluffy Rice & Grains

Rinse rice, 1:1½ ratio, tight lid, 18 minutes simmer, 10 minutes rest; finish with fork fluff and a knob of butter.

Experienced Hosting, timing, and show‑stoppers
Experienced

Hosting Flow & Timing

Build a prep timeline: day‑before desserts, morning sides, afternoon mains. Set the table early; keep a 170°F warm oven.

Experienced

Reverse‑Sear Steak

Bake at 250°F to 10°F below target, rest 10 minutes, then hard sear in cast iron with butter for edge‑to‑edge doneness.

Experienced

Whole Roast Chicken

Dry‑brine 12–24 hrs, roast at 425°F on a rack; baste once. Rest 10–15 min; carve and finish with pan juices.

Experienced

Plating & Hold Temps

Warm plates, rest meats, and use 140–170°F oven or warming drawer to hold sides so everything hits the table hot.
